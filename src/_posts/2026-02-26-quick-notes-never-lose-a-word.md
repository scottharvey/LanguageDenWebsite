We've all been there. You're reading a book, watching a movie, or having a conversation when you encounter a perfect new word. It's exactly what you've been looking for to express that specific idea. You think, "I need to remember this!"

But by the time you open your flashcard app, navigate through menus, create a new card, add translations... the moment is gone. Maybe you've forgotten the exact context. Maybe you can't remember how it was used. Or worst of all, maybe you've forgotten the word entirely.

This is why we built Quick Notes.

## The Problem with Traditional Flashcard Creation

Most flashcard apps treat card creation as a deliberate, focused activity. You sit down, open the app, and carefully craft each card with:

- The word or phrase
- Translation
- Example sentences
- Audio (if available)
- Images (if relevant)
- Tags and categories

This works great when you're in "study mode." But language learning doesn't just happen at your desk. It happens:

- On the subway while reading
- During your favorite Netflix show
- In conversations with native speakers
- While scrolling social media
- During work when you encounter technical terms

These spontaneous moments are often when you encounter the most useful, relevant vocabulary. But traditional apps make it too hard to capture them.

## Quick Notes: Capture Now, Organize Later

## The Quick Notes Workflow

Here's the recommended workflow that our most successful users follow:

### 1. Capture Everything

Don't self-censor. If a word seems even slightly interesting or useful, capture it. You can always delete it later, but you can't recover a word you've forgotten.

### 2. Weekly Review Sessions

Set aside 20-30 minutes each week to review your Quick Notes:

- Add translations and definitions
- Create example sentences
- Convert to full flashcards
- Delete words you've decided aren't useful
- Group related vocabulary

### 3. Use Voice Input

For even faster capture, use your phone's voice-to-text feature. This is especially useful when your hands are busy or when you're trying to capture pronunciation.

## Quick Notes vs. The Competition

Let's be honest about how other apps handle spontaneous vocabulary capture:

**Anki**: Requires you to create a complete card with front and back. No quick capture option. Many users resort to keeping separate notes apps just to capture words for later Anki entry.

**Quizlet**: Focused on pre-made sets. Adding individual words requires navigating to a specific set first. No dedicated quick capture feature.

**Duolingo**: Doesn't allow custom vocabulary at all. You're stuck with their curriculum whether those words are relevant to you or not.

**Traditional Note Apps**: Sure, you could use Apple Notes or Google Keep, but then you have to manually transfer everything to your flashcard app. Quick Notes eliminates this extra step.

## Pro Tips for Quick Notes

After analyzing how our most successful users leverage Quick Notes, here are the patterns we've noticed:

### 1. Add Context Clues

When you capture a word, add a tiny context hint:
- "madrugar - something about mornings"
- "schadenfreude - that feeling when..."
- "要是 - if/suppose?"

These hints help you remember why you saved the word when you review later.

### 2. Use Your Native Language

This isn't the time to practice. If you encounter a French word, feel free to write notes in English. The goal is speed and capture, not perfect target language use.

### 3. Capture Phrases, Not Just Words

Don't limit yourself to individual words. Quick Notes works great for:
- Idioms: "break a leg"
- Collocations: "make a decision" (not "do a decision")
- Sentence patterns: "the more... the better..."

### 4. Set a Regular Review Schedule

Quick Notes are only useful if you actually review them. Our data shows users who review weekly retain 73% more vocabulary than those who let notes pile up for months.

## The Psychology Behind Quick Notes

Quick Notes works because it respects how memory actually functions:

1. **The Generation Effect**: You're more likely to remember things you actively record versus passively read

2. **Reduced Cognitive Load**: By separating capture from organization, you can focus on understanding in the moment

3. **The Zeigarnik Effect**: Incomplete tasks (like unfleshed notes) create mental tension that aids memory

4. **Context-Dependent Memory**: Even minimal context notes help trigger fuller recall later
